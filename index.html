Star Classifier

The Infomation used to Identify your Star:

Mass

The mass of a Star can be measured in kilograms (kg), or it can be measured in Solar Masses (M). 1 Solar Mass is equal to the mass of the Sun, 1.98 x 1030kg.

Values for mass M can range from 0.08M to 60M, anything outside that range would be difficult to classify as a star.

Radius

The radius of a circle or sphere is usually measured in metres, but due to the large values of radii measured in stars, astronomers use R (the radius of the Sun) as a unit. The radius of our Sun (R) is 6.957x10 8m.
Values typically range from 0.1R to 100R, sometimes up to 2000R.

Temperature

Temperatures of active stars can be as low as 2400k (M-Type), or as high as 50,000K+ (O-Type).

Some Stars may have temperatures below 2400K, but they would not be considered as active, temperatures are not high enough for nuclear fusion to take place.

Absolute Magnitude

The absolute magnitude of a star is how bright the star would look if an observer were 10 parsecs (about 32.6 light years) away.

The apparent magnitude of a star depends on the intrinsic brightness and distance, it is a measure of how bright a star looks from Earth - not a good value for comparison with other stars.

Absolute magnitude (M) can be calculated if the apparent magnitude (m) and distance (d) (in parsecs) are known. The formula is: M = m - 5 log10(d/10).

The values of absolute magnitude can range from -8 to +15.

What does your Classification mean?

Spectral Class

O-Type

Very rare (<0.1% of stars), extremely luminous, short lived (only a few million years), typically blue/ blue-white. Show strong ionised helium absorption lines. Temperatures range from 25,000K and above.

B-Type

Uncommon, bright, typically blue-white. Sbow neutral helium lines and some hydrogen lines. Temperatures range from 11,000 - 25,0000K.

A-Type

More common than B-Type and O-Type, show very strong hydrogen lines. Typically white, sometimes bluish-white. Temperature range from 7,500 - 11,000K.

F-Type

Still hot, strong hydrogen and metal lines. These are slightly hotter than the Sun. Typically yellow-white. Temperatures range from 6,000-7,500K

G-Type

"Sun-like stars." Metal absorption lines (like iron, calcium) dominate. Typically yellow. Temperatures range from 5,200-6,000K

K-Type

Very common stars, longer lived than G-Type stars. Typically orange. Temperatures range from 3,700-5,200K.

M-Type

The most common type of star (>70%), includes red dwarfs and red giants. Strong molecular bands like titanium oxide. Typically red. Temperatures range from 2,400-3,700K.

Luminosity Class

V: Main Sequence Stars (Dwarfs).

These are "normal stars" that fuse hydrogen into helium in their cores. Stars spend most of their life as a Main Sequence star, making them the most common star type. Main Sequence Stars are smaller in size compared to Giants and Supergiant Stars.


III: Giants

These are stars that have finished core hydrogen fusion. They expand and cool as they start fusing helium or heavier elements. They have a much larger radius than Main Sequence Stars, making them appear brighter even if cooler.


I: Supergiants

These are massive stars near the end of their lives. They have an enormous radius (tens to thousands of solar radii), making them extremely luminous - they can outshine entire star clusters. Supergiants often end in Supernovae.

The Stellar Classification System

A star is classifed using two coordinates in the stellar classification system, spectral class and luminosity class. First the spectral class is identified using the tmeperature of the star. Then the mass, radius and absolute magnitude are compared alongside the typical ranges for each luminosity class. Once identified, the star's stellar classification can be determined (spectral class + luminosity class), e.g. "G V".
