Mass

The mass of a Star can be measured in kilograms (kg), or it can be measured in Solar Masses (M). 1 Solar Mass is equal to the mass of our Sun, 1.98 x 1030kg. Since stars are most commonly found in the range 0.1M to 1.0M, the unit chosen for mass in the form was the Solar Mass.

Radius

The radius of a star can vary from 0.1R to 2000R, where R is the radius of our Sun, 6.957x10 8m. The most common values lie within 0.1R - 1R, which are main sequence stars, the most common star in the universe. The form below takes radius as a multiple of R.

Temperature

Temperatures of active stars can be as low as 2400k (M-Type), or as high as 50,000+K (O-Type). The form below takes temperature in Kelvin.

Absolute Magnitude

The absolute magnitude of a star is how bright the star would look if an observer were 10 parsecs (about 32.6 light years) away. The apparent magnitude of a star depends on the intrinsic brightness and distance, it is a measure of how bright a star looks from Earth - not a good parameter for comparison. Absolute magnitude can be as low as -8, and as high as 15. The form below takes the value as it is. If the absolute magnitude is unknown, there is a separate calculator available for assistance.
